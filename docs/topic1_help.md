# Help Guide — Topic 1: AI Hallucinations in Summarization

> **Read this tonight. It will prepare you for every question a teacher might ask tomorrow.**

## First — What Is Your Research About? (In Very Simple Words)

Imagine you have a 10-page news article. You ask an AI to give you a short summary of it in 3 bullet points. The AI gives you the summary — but one of those bullet points says "The accident happened on March 5th" when the article actually says March 15th. The AI made up a wrong date. It did not get it from the document. It just invented it.

This is called a **hallucination** — when AI makes up facts that were never there.

Now imagine a hospital or a law firm using AI to summarize hundreds of important documents every day. One wrong fact could cause a very serious mistake.

**Your research tests this:** Do expensive, paid AI tools make fewer mistakes than free, open-source ones? And is the extra cost actually worth it?

That's it. You are putting a price tag on how trustworthy an AI is.

---

## What to Say When a Teacher Asks "What Is Your Research About?"

Say this:

> *"I am researching how often AI tools make up false information when summarizing documents. I want to compare free AI tools against expensive paid ones — measure how often each one makes mistakes, calculate what each one costs, and find out if paying more actually gives you more reliable results."*

Short. Clear. Confident. Done.

## Questions Teachers Will  Ask — With Simple Answers Ready

### ❓ "What do you mean by a hallucination in AI?"
**Say:** *"It is when the AI writes something in its summary that was never in the original document. For example, if the original article says a meeting happened in Lahore, but the AI summary says Karachi — that is a hallucination. The AI invented it."*

---

### ❓ "How will you find out if the AI made a mistake?"
**Say:** *"I will compare the AI's summary against the original document. If the summary contains any fact — a number, a name, a date — that cannot be found in the original text, I count it as a hallucination. I will do this for hundreds of documents and keep a record of every mistake."*

---

### ❓ "Why does this matter?"
**Say:** *"Because in important fields like law, medicine, and education, one wrong fact in a summary can lead to a very serious mistake. If an AI is writing summaries for doctors or lawyers, it must be trustworthy. My research helps organizations know which AI they can actually rely on — and what that reliability costs."*

---

### ❓ "How will you measure the cost?"
**Say:** *"The paid AI tools charge money every time you use them — like a pay-per-use service. I will check their billing records and calculate exactly how much it costs to summarize every 1,000 words. The free tools cost nothing to use, but I will also calculate the electricity and computing costs of running them."*

---

### ❓ "Did you use the same instructions for every AI tool?"
**Say:** *"Yes. Every single AI tool received the exact same instruction and the exact same document. The only thing that changed was which AI tool I used. This makes the comparison completely fair."*

---

### ❓ "Why compare paid and free AI tools?"
**Say:** *"Because this is the real choice that organizations face every day. Should they pay for an expensive AI service, or use a free one? My research gives them a real, data-based answer instead of just guessing."*

---

### ❓ "What documents will you use for testing?"
**Say:** *"I will use publicly available collections of news articles and documents that researchers already use for this kind of testing. They contain real, verifiable facts — which makes it easy to check if the AI made something up."*

---

### ❓ "Is this research good enough for a Master's thesis?"
**Say:** *"Yes. It involves designing a fair experiment, testing multiple AI systems, measuring and analyzing data, and producing results that are useful for real organizations. It contributes new, independent knowledge to the field of AI reliability — which is one of the most important topics in AI research right now."*

---

### ❓ "What if both types of AI tools make the same number of mistakes?"
**Say:** *"That would actually be a very important finding — it would mean that paying more money does not give you more accurate results. That challenges something many people currently believe, and it would be a valuable contribution to the research."*

---

### ❓ "Have you read any research papers on this?"
**Say:** *"Yes, I have reviewed studies on AI hallucination detection, summarization evaluation methods, and AI cost analysis. My full literature review will be completed as the first stage of the thesis work."*

## Key Words to Know (In Case They Come Up)

| Word | What It Means Simply |
|---|---|
| **Hallucination** | When AI writes a fact that was never in the original document — it invented it |
| **Summarization** | When AI reads a long document and gives you a short version of it |
| **Open-source AI** | Free AI tools that anyone can download and use |
| **Proprietary AI** | Paid AI tools owned by a company — you pay to use them |
| **Hallucination Rate** | Out of all the summaries made, what percentage had at least one made-up fact |
| **Token** | A small unit of text that AI reads and processes — roughly one word |
| **Prompt** | The instruction you give to the AI (e.g., "Summarize this in 3 bullet points") |
| **Benchmark** | A fair, standardized test used to compare different AI tools against each other |
| **Faithfulness** | Whether the AI's summary stays true to what was actually in the original document |
| **ROUGE Score** | A score used to measure how similar an AI summary is to the original — but it only checks word overlap, not whether facts are correct |

## Why This Is a Strong Thesis — Say This If Asked

1. **The results are numbers** — hallucination rate is a clear percentage, cost is a clear amount. No guessing.
2. **It challenges a common assumption** — that expensive AI is always more reliable.
3. **It is useful immediately** — any organization using AI for documents can use the findings to make a smarter decision.
4. **It is very relevant in 2026** — AI trust and reliability is one of the biggest conversations happening right now.
5. **It produces something practical** — a comparison table and cost guide that people can actually use.
