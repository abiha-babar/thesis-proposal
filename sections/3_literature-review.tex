\section{Literature Review}
\label{sec:literature}

\subsection{Existing Research}

Other researchers have already studied parts of this problem, and their work forms the starting point for this thesis.

First, studies have shown that AI can read and extract information from messy, unstructured documents like scanned forms and receipts. However, the accuracy depends a lot on the type of document and the AI model being used. Jaume et al.\ \cite{jaume2019funsd} created a well-known test collection of scanned administrative forms and showed that checking whether the AI got the exact right answer (yes or no) is the best way to measure accuracy for this kind of task. Park et al.\ \cite{park2019cord} did similar work using a collection of receipts and found the same thing. Extracting data correctly from real-world documents is genuinely difficult, and a proper test is needed to measure it.

Second, researchers have shown that large AI models can be made much smaller without losing much of their ability. Dettmers et al.\ \cite{dettmers2022llmint8} proved this in a detailed study, showing that a large AI can be compressed to use much less memory and still work well on a regular laptop. Frantar et al.\ \cite{frantar2022gptq} went even further, developing a method that compresses AI models even more efficiently. The most recent milestones in this direction are three architecturally distinct edge SLMs: Gemma~4 E4B (Google DeepMind, April 2026), which natively processes document images offline on a mobile device~\cite{gemma4_2026}; Phi-4 Mini Multimodal (Microsoft), which uses a Mixture-of-LoRAs strategy and multi-crop image splitting to handle complex layouts within a 3.8B parameter budget~\cite{phi4mini_2025}; and Qwen2.5-VL 3B (Alibaba), which leads the SLM class on structured Key Information Extraction through native spatial-coordinate reasoning~\cite{qwen25vl_2025}. Collectively, these studies and model releases prove that running a capable, document-aware AI on a normal laptop is not just theory but present-day reality---and that different local architectures prioritise different capabilities.

Third, Strubell et al.\ \cite{strubell2019energy} studied how much energy and money it costs to run AI systems and showed a clear way to calculate and compare those costs. This thesis will use a similar approach to compare the cost of running a local AI tool against the cost of an online AI service. Wei et al.\ \cite{wei2022emergent} also studied the general abilities and limits of large AI models, which helps explain why some models perform better than others on specific tasks.

\subsection{Research Gap}

Even though all of these areas have been studied, nobody has put them all together in one place. In particular, no study has compared \emph{multiple} on-device SLMs---with architecturally different designs---against a cloud API on the same task, at the same time, measuring accuracy, latency, and cost together. Evaluating only a single local model risks conflating the limitations of that specific architecture with the limitations of local deployment in general, undermining the generalisability of any conclusion. Furthermore, no prior work systematically examines whether extraction results change depending on how the instruction is phrased, even though LLM outputs are well known to be sensitive to prompt wording. This thesis fills both gaps.

\subsection{Research Questions}

Based on everything studied above, this thesis will answer these three specific questions:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Question 1:} Is there a real, meaningful difference in accuracy between on-device SLMs (Gemma~4 E4B, Phi-4 Mini, Qwen2.5-VL~3B) and a paid cloud LLM when extracting information from the same documents, and do those accuracy differences remain consistent across multiple prompt variants?
    \item \textbf{Question 2:} How much faster or slower is each local SLM compared to the cloud service in terms of end-to-end document processing latency?
    \item \textbf{Question 3:} At what daily document volume does it become cheaper to use each local SLM instead of paying for the cloud service, given a reproducible cost model based on measured energy consumption and straight-line hardware depreciation?
\end{itemize}
